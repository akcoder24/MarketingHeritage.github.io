Location of the place

Deep in Jharkhand’s hinterland lies the village of Maluti with around 70 beautiful clay temples dating from the 17th century. They are now being restored. Maluti is a small village in Jharkhand's hinterland. Narrow pathways, flanked by mud dwellings and a few concrete structures, lead to many open spaces in the heart of the village, spaces that give witness to more than 300 years of beautiful temple construction. Once home to 108 temples, the village is now but a poor shadow of its past, with only 72 shrines remaining — but the sheer number puts Maluti on the heritage map of India. With intricate terracotta carvings of mythical scenes on their façades and walls, which have survived harsh weather and the ravages of time since the early 17th century, Maluti’s terracotta temples are now being restored. Situated in the Dumka district, the village is close to the border with West Bengal, and Chala architecture, inspired by traditional Bengali huts with their sloping roofs with curved edges, is a distinct feature of Maluti’s temples. Also, the influence of the exquisite Keshta Raya terracotta temple at Bishnupur in Bengal’s Bankura district can be seen clearly in every temple. The 17th-century masonry technique using moulded bricks has been applied extensively.


History of the place

The village, which once had 108 temples, is now merely a pale shade of its former self, with only 72 shrines standing — but their sheer number places Maluti on India's historical map. Maluti's terracotta temples are being repaired, with beautiful terracotta sculptures of mythical images on their façades and walls that have weathered harsh weather and the ravages of time since the early 17th century. Maluti village came into the limelight in the fifteenth century as the capital of nankar raj (tax-free kingdom.) The kingdom was awarded to one Basanta Roy of village Katigram by Sultan Alauddin Hussain Shah of Gaura (1495–1525). Son of a poor Brahmin Basanta managed to catch the pet hawk of the sultan and gave it back to the sultan. In lieu of the hawk (Baj), Basanta was given the kingdom. Hence, the king was called Raja Baj Basanta. The capital of the Baj Basanta dynasty was Damra. Later it was shifted to Maluti. The royal family was very pious.


Folklore of the place

The kingdom was awarded to one Basanta Roy of village Katigram by Sultan Alauddin Hussain Shah of Gauḍa (1495–1525). Son of a poor Brahmin Basanta managed to catch the pet hawk of the sultan and gave it back to the sultan. In exchange for the hawk (Baj), Basanta was given the kingdom. Hence, the king was called Raja Baj Basanta. Basanta became a king in lieu of a baj (hawk) with the help of a Dandi Sanyasi of Sumeru Math, Kashi may be true to a great extent because the word Baj has been pre-fixed with the name of Basanta to commemorate the event. The name Baj Basanta is comparatively prominent because it can be found both in local history and government records. Swamiji, the head of Sumeru Math, Varanasi, was the preceptor of Basanta. Since then the head of Sumeru Math who is called Rajguru become the preceptor of descendants of king Baj Basanta. Even today Rajguru from Sumeru Math Varanasi spends some time at Maluti every year.


Speciality of the place

Sikir Taraf, Rajar Bari, Madhya Bari, Chhoi Taraf, and the Mauliksha temple complex are the five clusters where Shiva temples can be found. Temples showcase Chala style, which is inspired by traditional Bengali huts with their sloping roofs and curved edges. Every temple also bears the imprint of the magnificent Keshta Raya terracotta temple in Bishnupur, Bengal's Bankura district. The use of moulded bricks, a 17th-century construction method, has been widely used. At the Madhyam Bari, there are meticulous depictions of the conflict scenario between Ram and Ravana. These scenes can also be found in Maluti's other temples. There are also ceramic panels depicting the life stories of Sri Krishna and Goddess Durga.



Conclusion

The 108 terracotta temples that were built from a gift from the king remains antique and ethenic despite being on the verge of destruction. The pity of these temples is that due to improper restoration out of 108 temples only 78 are left in a stable state. The sculptures and the statues here have already lost their structure and are slowly losing their beauty. The temples that could win a beautiful place couldn't win against time.
